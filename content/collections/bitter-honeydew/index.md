There was a time when everything tasted sweet. Soft light. Gentle air. The kind of quiet that tricks you into believing it will last.

And then there was Gretchen.

No one ever sees betrayal arriving the way it truly does—dressed not in darkness, but in familiarity. In laughter. In promises that feel too warm to question. Gretchen didn’t break things loudly. She unraveled them carefully, thread by thread, with a kind of precision that almost felt playful. Whimsical, even. Like a storybook character who smiles as she poisons the well.

<!--more-->

**Bitter Honeydew** lives in that space—where sweetness turns, but never all at once. These paintings carry the echo of that unraveling. Moments where trust collapses into something surreal. Where memory distorts just enough to make you question what was real and what was performed. There’s a quiet spite woven through the narrative—not explosive, but deliberate. The kind that lingers. The kind that follows you out of the story and into your life.

What began as something almost theatrical—an intimate betrayal, a personal undoing—bled outward into real-world consequence. Relationships fractured. Identity shifted. The ground beneath everything familiar gave way. And yet… this is not a story about staying there.

Where earlier works lived in restraint—muted, cold, suffocated—Bitter Honeydew breathes differently. Pigments push further. Greens are sharper. Yellows glow with an almost defiant warmth. Hues clash, then harmonize, as if relearning how to exist together. This is color not just as atmosphere, but as recovery.
Not naive. Not untouched. But reclaimed.

There is something unsettling in that vibrancy. It doesn’t ignore what happened—it survives it. The sweetness returns, but altered. No longer innocent. No longer fragile. It carries the bitterness with it, transformed into something strangely beautiful.
