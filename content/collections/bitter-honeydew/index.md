Bitter Honeydew unfolds like a story you’re not entirely sure you were meant to overhear. There’s a theatricality to it—moments that feel staged, exaggerated, almost playful—yet threaded with something more grounded, more real, quietly pressing through the surface.

The collection drifts between whimsy and tension, where gestures feel deliberate but motives remain just out of reach. Characters seem to linger in unresolved exchanges, caught somewhere between confession and performance. There’s drama here, but it doesn’t announce itself loudly—it simmers, twists, and occasionally slips into something absurd.

_This collection is in a planned work of progress._

<!--more-->

What makes Bitter Honeydew compelling for collectors is this constant push and pull: the surreal bending toward realism, and realism dissolving back into something stranger. Familiar forms begin to feel uncertain. Emotional cues blur. You’re left questioning what’s genuine, what’s exaggerated, and whether that distinction matters at all.

Each piece feels like a fragment of a larger narrative—one that refuses to fully explain itself, inviting you instead to sit with the ambiguity, to revisit, and to reinterpret. Over time, the collection reveals new tensions and quiet contradictions, rewarding those willing to engage beyond the first glance.
It’s a body of work that doesn’t settle. And that’s precisely where its intrigue lies.

Where earlier works lived in restraint—muted, cold, suffocated—Bitter Honeydew breathes differently. Pigments push further. Greens are sharper. Yellows glow with an almost defiant warmth. Hues clash, then harmonize, as if relearning how to exist together. This is color not just as atmosphere, but as recovery.
Not naive. Not untouched. But reclaimed.

There is something unsettling in that vibrancy. It doesn’t ignore what happened—it survives it. The sweetness returns, but altered. No longer innocent. No longer fragile. It carries the bitterness with it, transformed into something strangely beautiful.
